# NextStep Blog — Writing Style Guide

Content on this blog is written for experienced management consultants. They are smart, direct, and have low tolerance for fluff. Write accordingly.

---

## Voice

- Direct and specific. Say what you mean in the fewest words.
- Confident but not performative. Avoid hype.
- Peer-to-peer tone — not a coach, not a brand, not a mentor. A knowledgeable colleague.

---

## Hard Rules — Never Use

### 1. Em dashes
Do not use em dashes (—) in any form. Use a comma, period, or rewrite the sentence.

**Wrong:** The process is simple — and it works.
**Right:** The process is simple. It works.

### 2. "No X. No Y. Just Z." constructions
This is a common AI writing pattern that signals low-effort copy.

**Wrong:** No fluff. No jargon. Just results.
**Wrong:** No cold outreach. No job boards. Just warm introductions.
**Right:** The approach relies entirely on warm introductions.

### 3. "It's not X. It's Y." constructions
Another AI-signature contrast pattern. Avoid entirely.

**Wrong:** It's not about your resume. It's about your network.
**Wrong:** This isn't career advice. It's a framework for decision-making.
**Right:** Your network matters more than your resume in most consulting exits.

### 4. Hollow openers
Do not open articles or sections with these phrases:
- "In today's [competitive landscape / fast-changing world / job market]..."
- "Now more than ever..."
- "Whether you're a [X] or a [Y]..."
- "If you're reading this, chances are..."

### 5. Filler intensifiers
Remove on sight:
- "game-changer", "game-changing"
- "transformative", "transformational"
- "crucial", "essential", "vital" (unless genuinely true and specific)
- "leverage" used as a verb (say "use" instead)
- "delve into"
- "unlock"
- "navigate" used metaphorically ("navigate your career")
- "at the end of the day"
- "in conclusion" as a section opener

### 6. Three-word stacked sentences for dramatic effect
**Wrong:** You have options. Real options. Options that matter.
**Right:** You have more options than most people realize.

---

## Punctuation

- Use commas and periods. If a sentence needs an em dash, rewrite it.
- Avoid ellipses (...) except inside direct quotes.
- Serial comma always: "strategy, operations, and product."

---

## Numbers and Statistics

- Always cite the source inline if quoting a specific statistic.
- If the data comes from the NextStep Consulting Survey 2025, attribute it: "According to the NextStep Consulting Survey 2025..."
- Round numbers need context: "roughly 70%" not just "70%."
- Avoid presenting plausible-but-unverified figures as facts.

---

## Sentence Length

Mix short and medium sentences. Avoid both walls of text and staccato fragments used for effect.

**Avoid:** "Consulting exits are hard. Really hard. The hardest thing you'll do."
**Prefer:** "Consulting exits are harder than most people expect — not because the skills don't transfer, but because the process is opaque."

Wait. That last example used an em dash. Fix it:
**Right:** "Consulting exits are harder than most people expect. The skills transfer well; the process is what trips people up."

---

## What Good Looks Like

- Specific examples over vague generalizations
- Named tools, companies, and timelines over abstract advice
- One clear action per paragraph
- Headings that tell you what you'll learn, not just the topic
